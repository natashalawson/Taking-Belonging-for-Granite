The Problem
Geoscience's Diversity Crisis
Geoscience is one of the least racially diverse fields in STEM. Underrepresented racial minority groups comprise just 7% of the field a number unchanged over 40 years. Hostile climates, the field's entanglement with colonial resource extraction, and structural barriers at every level continue to drive diverse scientists out before they can change the field from within.
7% underrepresented groups in geoscience (Wilson, 2017)
50 yrs to gender parity at current rates (Bernard & Cooperdock, 2018)
The Gap
What We Don't Yet Know
Existing diversity initiatives focus on recruitment and retention but leave largely unexamined how geoscience PhD students understand and narrate their own intersecting identities. Without this knowledge, we cannot build the cultural conditions that make belonging sustainable rather than exceptional.
Little is known about how PhD geoscientists construct their intersecting identities, or how asset-based frameworks might reshape that self-understanding over time.
This study fills that gap by examining how a year-long PD program shapes how graduate scientists see themselves.
Preliminary Findings
A Surprising Identity Gap
Early observations reveal a striking and consistent tension in how geoscience PhD students narrate their own identities:
Participants do not identify as scientists despite years of active scientific training and research.
They believe they must be experts before they can educate others creating a double barrier to both scientific and educator identities.
The gap between what participants do every day and who they believe themselves to be suggests that diversifying geoscience requires more than changing who enters the field. It requires transforming how scientists understand themselves.
Identity recognition not just representation is the lever for change.

Research Questions
What This Study Asks
1
How does a year-long professional development program modeling Culturally Sustaining Pedagogy shape geoscience PhD students' understanding of themselves as learners, educators, and scientists?
2
In what ways do asset-based frameworks inform geoscience PhD students' capacity to become advocates for equity and inclusion within their fields and communities?

Methodology
Design-Based Research using Narrative Analysis
Background questionnaires baseline identity & context
Pre-interviews "Tell me about your journey as a science learner"
Monthly PD sessions CSP modeled, recorded + field notes
Digital reflective handouts ongoing identity documentation
Post-interviews narrative change over time
Thematic analysis in Dedoose intersectional frame (Crenshaw, 1989)
Purposive sampling Qualitative Year-long
Why This Matters
Significance for the Field
If PhD geoscientists cannot see themselves as scientists or educators, no amount of recruitment can build an inclusive field. Lasting change requires transforming the cultural conditions of graduate science education starting with how scientists understand themselves.
"We will limit the science we do if we do not become more inclusive. We need to do better." — Bernard & Cooperdock, 2018
